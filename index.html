Cultural Appropriation
the act of taking or using things from a culture that is not your own, especially without showing that you understand or respect this culture. [1]
Dominant Culture & Oppresion

Dominant culture is the most visible and accepted culture within a particular society. Oppression describes repeated and prolonged discrimination. It’s not just one-on-one behaviour, but a form of structural discrimination, often backed by powerful authorities. Racism, homophobia and sexism are all forms of oppression. [2]

Case 1
plagiarism and ownership issues

+
UK Brand KTZ —
Accused of copying Canadian Inuit Clothing

The sweater in question was a sacred design by a Canadian Inuit Shaman who “has been used and violated” in his life, according to his great-granddaughter Salome Awa. She claims KTZ neglected to mention the great-grandfather of whom the design was stolen, nor was there any offer of monetary gain for the victimized family.

What's the issue with it?

Although KTZ claimed the design was "inspired by and created to pay homage to indigenous cultures and tribes around the world”, they have failed to acknowledge or give credit the Inuit culture.[3] Cultural appropriation has a habit of giving the dominant group credit for aspects of a culture that they have taken, reinforcing the power imbalance between the two groups.[4]

MLB Cleveland Indians & NFL Kansas City — Controversial representation of Indigenous culture in Sports.

Chief Wahoo (left), a cartoonish caricature of a Native American, has long been the source of anguish and frustration for those who consider it offensive, outdated and racist. The team stopped using the Chief Wahoo logo on their uniforms beginning in 2019, according to MLB. [5]

NFL Kansas City Chiefs fan (right) was caught dressed in a feathered Indian war bonnet during the AFC Championship game against the Tennessee Titans at Arrowhead Stadium on January 19, 2020 in Kansas City, Missouri.

What's the issue with it?

Cultural appropriation often adds to stereotypes faced by non-dominant cultures. When people from dominant cultures ‘dress up’ or 'mis-represent' these cultures without knowing the context, cultural significance of the costume and keeps these stereotypes going. [3]

Case 3
Offensive Cultural References

+
Malfi The Penguin —
Moncler issues apology over its ‘blackface’ collection

The new logo appears to take inspiration from the face of a 19th-century golliwog doll. For those who aren’t aware, these controversial toys – known for their black skin, clown lips, and frizzy hair – are widely censured in the UK due to their offensive, racist undertones. The brand has since issued an apology for the misunderstanding; assuring customers that the stylised face was not actually a golliwog – but instead, a friendly penguin called Malfi. [6]

What's the issue with it?

Moncler used traits – black skin, white googly eyes, and red clown lip – that are commonly seen in the 'blackface' depiction. Blackface isn't just about painting one's skin darker or putting on a costume. It invokes a racist and painful history.[7] Although they claim to be accidental, it is their responsibility to be more careful about cultural sensitivity.

Addressing the issues of cultural appropriation doesn't mean that your freedom of creativity is restricted.
